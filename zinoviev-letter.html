Zinoviev Letter: The Rise of Labour and The Red Scare

Abdul Rehman
Islamia College, Peshawar
Published 1 November 2024 7 min read

Fourteen years have passed since the last Labour Government, led by former Prime Minister Gordon Brown, from 2007 to 2010. Sir Keir Starmer, the Leader of the Labour Party, has achieved a landslide victory over the Conservatives and has been elected as the new Prime Minister of the United Kingdom. This election marks the Conservative Party's worst electoral defeat in its history. The First Labour Government, formed in 1924, ended abruptly in November of the same year, and this event remains a significant part of Labour Party mythology, continuing to haunt British politics even today.1

The Great War and The Decline of The Aristocratic Class

The Great War, known as the war to end all wars, was characterised by brutal trench warfare and the use of poisonous gas. The introduction of modern military vehicles such as tanks and aeroplanes made the war extremely costly in lives lost. While the exact number of casualties remains uncertain, most sources report that over 8 million soldiers died in WW1. However, the two outbreaks of Spanish Flu in 1918 further complicated the records.2 Trench warfare, a defining feature of the conflict, involved soldiers from opposing sides digging in to protect themselves. It was brutal; soldiers often gained only a few metres of ground for great losses. Life in the trenches was exceedingly stressful due to the loud sounds of artillery targeting their positions, as well as poor hygiene and the spread of disease by rats and lice. The conditions in trench warfare were inhumane, and the poisonous clouds only added to the hardships.3 The war resulted in the loss of many members of the British ruling class, leaving many families without heirs to carry on their legacy. It had a profound effect on the socio-economic structure of British society.4 The transfer of power to the general public significantly impacted the ruling class, as individuals from lower social strata rose to positions of authority. This shift in the British class structure made the aristocracy more vulnerable to new forms of accountability. As entire lineages vanished, estates passed out of aristocratic hands — creating a power vacuum that returning veterans and their trade-union allies were primed to fill.

Communism Gains Traction

The trenches left veterans hungry not just for reform, but for deeper change. Karl Marx's century-old critique of industrial capitalism now resonated with ex-servicemen. Marxist communist ideologies gained traction following the Bolshevik revolution in 1917, which led to the abdication of the Czars. Capitalising on anti-war sentiment, Vladimir Lenin successfully overthrew the Provisional Government and ultimately withdrew Russia from the war.5 The global spread of Communist ideologies during the post-Great War period was facilitated by the favourable environment that followed the war.6 The creation of the Third International, known as COMINTERN, by Vladimir Lenin played a key role in supporting the worldwide communist revolution. This international organisation promoted the spread of communism and backed communist parties across various nations, effectively representing the interests of the USSR.7 The formation of the Communist Party of Great Britain (CPGB) was inspired by the revolution in Eastern Europe: the CPGB aimed to emulate the Bolshevik Revolution and overthrow the capitalist system in Britain.8 Like many other communist parties in Europe and beyond, the CPGB was closely linked with COMINTERN and was heavily influenced by it, especially through figures such as Bob Stewart, Albert Inkpen, John McLean, and others.9 10 11

Labour's Rise and The Zinoviev Letter

The Labour Party's gradual rise, its alliance with the Trade Union Congress, and its overall pledge to improve the lives of the working class and ensure their representation resonated with individuals who felt neglected by their existing representation. The decline of the Liberal Party at that period boosted Labour, and Arthur Henderson's leadership demonstrated Labour's capacity to act independently.12 The emergence of the Labour Party as a key force in British politics made it a formidable opponent to the Conservatives, eventually resulting in a government formed with the support of Asquith and his Liberals. During this time, Ramsay MacDonald's government introduced various laws and policies aimed at improving housing, education, and tackling unemployment. However, lacking a clear majority, they struggled to pass socialist legislation aligned with their agenda. They were in office from January to November 1924, which limited their ability to pass new laws. During that time Labour government officially recognised the Soviets in February 1924 through a series of concessions exchanged for ambassadors. This decision faced strong opposition from the Conservatives, who opposed engaging with the Soviets and instead supported a policy of isolation. The Conservatives viewed the Soviets as a significant socio-political threat, especially after the formation of COMINTERN, which aimed to promote a global communist revolution.13 Just before the 1924 elections, the Daily Mail published a letter purportedly written by Grigory Zinoviev, the head of COMINTERN, urging the CPGB to carry out seditious activities to ignite a Bolshevik proletarian revolution in Britain.14 Although Zinoviev denied the letter's authenticity, it influenced public perception of the Labour Party during the 1924 election. Consequently, the Labour Party was perceived as sympathetic to the USSR and communist ideologies. Supporters of Labour claimed that their defeat in the 1924 general elections was influenced by the Zinoviev Letter.

Legacy and Lessons

Following the formation of Stanley Baldwin's government, a committee was established to examine the letter, ultimately confirming its authenticity.15 However, no further investigations followed, and historians widely agree that it was a forgery. It is important to note that the Labour Party's loss cannot be solely attributed to the Zinoviev Letter; other factors, including their policies and the political climate at the time, also played a role. Elections happen around the world, prompting us to reflect on past electoral disputes where even minor errors could influence the outcome. This highlights the importance of transparent communication between political parties. Active participation in democratic processes is crucial to counter misinformation and propaganda, which can influence public opinion. This is especially vital given the rising cyber threats and false information in our interconnected global community, where individuals can be misled by fabricated stories designed to serve personal interests.
