A Climate of Performative Spectacles: Why Political Theatre Has Replaced Genuine Governance

As democratic politics increasingly becomes a performance optimised for media consumption rather than policy delivery, this piece argues that the gap between political spectacle and substantive governance has become one of the defining crises of contemporary democracy — and asks what responsibility citizens bear for demanding better.

Political TheatreDemocratic Erosion Media PoliticsPopulismGovernance

The Performance Has Replaced the Policy

We are living through an era in which the image of action has become more politically valuable than action itself. Governments announce programmes that are never implemented, hold summits that produce communiqués but not change, and stage confrontations designed for social media virality rather than policy impact.

"Politics has always involved performance. What is new is when performance becomes the substance, not the packaging."

The Infrastructure of Spectacle

Several structural forces have converged to create the current climate. The fragmentation of media has shortened the attention cycle to the point where governments are rewarded for generating news rather than making progress. Social media platforms algorithmically amplify outrage and conflict over measured deliberation. The result is a feedback loop: politicians perform because it works; it works because media amplifies it; media amplifies it because audiences engage; and audiences engage because they have been trained to expect drama, not deliberation.

Pakistan as a Case Study

Pakistan's recent political cycle offers an instructive, if painful, illustration. The years 2021–2024 saw extraordinary political theatre — mass rallies, dramatic resignations, constitutional crises, and sweeping counter-narratives — but very little policy substance. Economic management during this period was characterised more by blame-shifting than reform. What united all political actors was the prioritisation of optics over outcomes.

The Citizen's Complicity

It would be comfortable to attribute the climate of performative politics entirely to cynical elites. But this lets citizens off too easily. Audiences reward spectacle. They share clips of rhetorical victories rather than analyses of policy failures. Democratic politics, ultimately, produces the leaders that democratic publics are willing to accept.

Conclusion

The climate of performative spectacles is not inevitable. It is the product of specific structural choices — in media, in political incentives, in civic culture — that can be made differently. But changing them requires first seeing them clearly, and resisting the seduction of the performance.

Understanding the Dilemma of District Kurram: Extension of a New Social Contract Without Mainstreaming FATA

From the deadly sectarian conflict of 2024–2025 rooted in tribal land disputes, to the broader failure of the 25th Amendment's promise of mainstreaming, this piece traces the political and humanitarian unravelling of Kurram district — and argues that without a genuine new social contract, imposed peace will remain fragile and temporary.

Kurram DistrictFATA Merger 25th AmendmentTribal GovernanceSectarianism

The Crisis of 2024–2025

Between late 2024 and early 2025, District Kurram in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa became the site of some of Pakistan's most deadly inter-communal violence in years. Clashes rooted in longstanding tribal land disputes between Sunni and Shia communities left hundreds dead and tens of thousands displaced. The Pakistani state's response was characterised by delay, inadequacy, and an apparent inability to enforce basic security in its own territory.

The Promise of the 25th Amendment

The 25th Constitutional Amendment, passed in 2018, merged the Federally Administered Tribal Areas (FATA) with Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, formally extending the writ of the Pakistani state. The ambition was admirable. The implementation was catastrophic. The merger created a legal and administrative jurisdiction on paper without building the institutional infrastructure to make it real on the ground. Courts were declared competent before they had judges who understood tribal land tenure. Police were deployed before they had the local legitimacy or numerical strength to enforce order.

Land, Identity, and the Roots of Conflict

The specific conflict in Kurram is inseparable from disputes over agricultural land and grazing rights that have existed for generations. It would be a serious analytical error to reduce the conflict to sectarianism alone. The sectarian framing often serves the interests of those who wish to avoid accountability for the failure of land settlement and dispute resolution.

"What is called a sectarian conflict is very often a resource conflict in sectarian clothing."

Toward a Genuine Social Contract

What Kurram requires is not simply better security deployment or faster court construction. It requires a genuine renegotiation of the social contract between the Pakistani state and these communities: one that acknowledges the legitimacy of customary institutions where they function, builds formal state capacity alongside rather than instead of them, and creates genuine political representation.

Conclusion

The tragedy of Kurram is the consequence of a structural reform — the FATA merger — that was implemented without the seriousness of purpose it required. The promise of mainstreaming has not been kept. Until it is, the violence will recur, and the state's claim to sovereignty in its own territory will remain a legal fiction rather than a lived reality.
